Why our brains are wired to believe false information, and what actually works to stop it

Original Paper: Towards the study of world misinformation

Authors: Ronzani, P.
Journal: Harvard Kennedy School Misinformation Review
Year: 2025
Read the original paper

Misinformation spreads rapidly, especially online, and often influences our beliefs and decisions. It's not just about lacking information; our brains are wired in ways that can make us susceptible to believing false narratives, particularly those that align with our existing views or emotions. Understanding these psychological mechanisms is key to developing effective strategies to counter the spread and impact of misinformation.

What the researchers did:

This paper reviews the current landscape of misinformation research, focusing on global trends and the psychological underpinnings of why people believe and share false information. It synthesizes findings from various studies to identify common factors that make individuals vulnerable to misinformation and explores different intervention strategies that have been tested to mitigate its effects, particularly in diverse cultural contexts.

What they found:

  • People are more likely to believe misinformation that confirms their existing beliefs (confirmation bias).
  • Emotional content, especially fear or anger, makes misinformation more shareable and believable.
  • Repeated exposure to false information can make it seem more truthful over time (illusory truth effect).
  • Interventions that focus on pre-bunking (inoculating people against misinformation before they encounter it) are more effective than post-bunking (correcting misinformation after it has spread).
  • Fact-checking can be effective, but its impact is often limited by people's existing biases and the speed at which misinformation spreads.
  • Digital literacy and critical thinking skills are crucial for individuals to navigate the complex information landscape.

Imagine your brain is like a garden. Misinformation is like a weed seed that blows in. If your garden (brain) is already full of similar plants (existing beliefs) or if the soil is particularly fertile for that type of weed (strong emotions), the weed seed will take root easily and grow quickly. Trying to pull out every weed after it has grown (post-bunking) is hard and time-consuming. A more effective strategy is to prepare the soil and plant strong, healthy plants (critical thinking, media literacy) that can resist the weeds from taking root in the first place (pre-bunking).

What this means for you:

This research emphasizes that combating misinformation requires a multi-faceted approach. For individuals, it means actively cultivating critical thinking skills, being aware of cognitive biases, and questioning emotionally charged content. For platforms and policymakers, it suggests prioritizing interventions that build resilience against misinformation, rather than solely focusing on reactive fact-checking. Understanding how our brains process information is the first step towards a more informed society.

Key terms glossary:

Misinformation
False or inaccurate information, especially that which is intended to deceive.
Confirmation Bias
The tendency to interpret new evidence as confirmation of one's existing beliefs or theories.
Illusory Truth Effect
The tendency to believe information to be correct after repeated exposure, even if it is false.
Pre-bunking (Inoculation)
A strategy to build resistance against misinformation by exposing individuals to weakened forms of misleading arguments beforehand.
